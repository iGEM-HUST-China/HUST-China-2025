Our Innovation

Upcycling Waste to Beauty

We use synthetic biology to upgrade waste cooking oil (WCO) into cosmetic-grade squalane, protecting marine ecosystems while enabling low-carbon supply chains.

Key Advantages

Upcycling: Using waste cooking oil as raw material reduces costs and carbon footprint.

Green Alternative: Replaces traditional shark liver sources, protecting marine biodiversity.

Efficient Chassis: Engineered Yarrowia lipolytica for targeted squalane synthesis.

Industry Integration: Connects with cosmetics and vaccine adjuvant applications.

Waste Oil Crisis

China produces approximately 11-12 million tons of waste cooking oil annually. Improper handling not only causes serious food safety risks (illegal re-entry into the food chain) but also leads to environmental pollution (water eutrophication, odorous gas emissions) and public health risks.

75% Water pollution
60% Soil degradation
40% Marine life impact

Current Disposal Methods

Incineration/Power Generation: High energy consumption, pollutant emissions

Conversion to Biodiesel: Relatively mature technology but with high industry concentration and cost pressures

Simple Physical Recovery/Landfilling: Prone to secondary pollution, poor economic benefits

Our Solution

Chassis: Engineered Yarrowia lipolytica, lipid metabolism friendly, strong tolerance

Raw Material Processing: Expressing lipases to decompose oils, releasing free fatty acids (FFA) and glycerol into the metabolic network

Flux Redirection:

  • MVA pathway enhancement: Upregulation of key nodes like HMG-CoA reductase
  • Compartmentalization strategy: Anchoring key enzymes/pathway components in peroxisomes and lipid droplets
  • Energy and reducing power optimization: Enhancing NADPH/ATP supply
  • Stress resistance and homeostasis: Enhancing solvent/lipid load tolerance

Supporting Work

Modeling: Flux balance and key parameter sensitivity analysis

Hardware: Pretreatment modules, online monitoring and cost assessment

Human Practices & Education: Dialogue and科普 with industry, public and regulators
